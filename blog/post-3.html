Yoga on Water: When a Great Idea Meets Reality

By Janis · 3 min read

I remember watching yoga instructors wobble on SUP boards in the middle of Wisdom Valley's lake, trying to hold downward dog while the water pushed back. It looked incredible. Peaceful. The kind of thing that makes people stop and stare. And I thought: This is it. This is the next wave.

Spoiler: it wasn't.

When the surfskate trend started cooling off, I wasn't going to sit around. I wanted something new ‚Äî something that could use the same community space but offer something different. Stand-up paddleboarding caught my attention. Wisdom Valley had this large lake just sitting there. Perfect for yoga on water. Aerial shots. Social media gold. I could see it.

So I ordered boards. Hard boards. Inflatable boards. All from China, just to test. Found potential yoga instructors and put them through training. Reached out to Nok Studio, a local yoga studio, to partner. We pulled off a big show on the water ‚Äî multiple times. It looked stunning. We even hosted SUP training for a rugby school's summer program. Kids paddling. Parents watching. Everything felt aligned.

But here's what I learned: a beautiful idea doesn't fix logistics.

SUP yoga relies on one thing: water. Not just any water ‚Äî accessible, usable water. Wisdom Valley's lake? Government approval needed. Private artificial lakes? Owner permission required. Hotels? They care more about their guests relaxing than some entrepreneur running a niche water sport in their backyard. Every water source came with a gatekeeper. And every gatekeeper said no, or made it so difficult that profitability disappeared.

I looked at the numbers. Short term? No profit. Long term? Only viable as an add-on to something else, not a standalone business. So I made the call: end the project. Quickly. Cleanly. No dragging it out hoping it would magically work.

Sometimes the best business decision is knowing when to stop. Not every idea deserves a year of your life. Not every beautiful concept translates into a sustainable model. And that's okay. You test, you learn, you move on.

The SUP boards? They're still somewhere in storage. But the lesson? That I keep.
